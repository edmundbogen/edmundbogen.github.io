Big John: How One Tower Reinvented
Life in the Sky

Architecture & Living
By Carrie McCormick  ·  June 3, 2026

Every home has a story. Some have a skyline. And exactly one of them taught an entire city that you could raise a family, run a business, and watch the lake turn silver at dusk — all from a thousand feet above Michigan Avenue.

I have spent twenty-five years selling Chicago — its Gold Coast brownstones, its glass towers over the river, its quiet Lincoln Park streets. And in all that time, no building has held more of the city's imagination than the dark, tapered tower my clients still call Big John.

Officially, it is 875 North Michigan Avenue. To the rest of the world, it will always be the John Hancock Center — and to me, it is the clearest answer I can give when a buyer asks what makes Chicago different from anywhere else. Let me bring its story to life the way I would walk you through a home.

The tower that wore its strength on the outside

When the Hancock topped out on May 6, 1968, it was the second-tallest building in the world, behind only the Empire State Building. But height was never the point. The point was the idea inside it.

That idea belonged to two men at Skidmore, Owings & Merrill: architect Bruce Graham and a structural engineer named Fazlur Rahman Khan — arguably the most important skyscraper mind of the twentieth century. Khan understood that a tall building did not need a heavy interior skeleton fighting the wind floor by floor. Instead, he turned the skin of the tower into its strength: the now-famous X-braces you see from blocks away are not decoration. They are the structure.

It was called the trussed-tube system, and the Hancock was the first building on earth to use it. The payoff was extraordinary. By moving the muscle to the exterior, Khan and Graham freed the floors inside — wider spans, fewer columns, taller windows, more light. They had not just built something tall. They had invented a way of building that the entire world would copy for the next fifty years.

"Most towers hide their strength. Big John wears it on the outside — and that honesty is exactly why Chicago fell in love with it."

The world's first true vertical neighborhood

Here is the detail I love most, the one I always save for the top of the elevator ride. The Hancock was the world's first mixed-use supertall — a single tower designed to be an entire neighborhood stacked on itself.

Retail at the base. Offices in the middle. And then, in the sky, roughly 700 residences — including, at the time it opened, the highest homes in the world. People did not just work in Big John. They lived there. They woke up above the clouds, sent their children to school at street level, and came home to a view that no amount of money can manufacture anywhere else in this city.

That was a radical thought in 1969, and it is the very thought that defines luxury living in Chicago today. Every glass condo tower along the river, every full-amenity address my clients covet — they are all descendants of the experiment Khan and Graham ran in this one black tower on Michigan Avenue.

The name on the door, and the name in our hearts

In February 2018, the building quietly lost its famous name. John Hancock Insurance had not held a naming-rights deal since 2013, and when the company asked for its name to come down, the tower became, simply, its own address: 875 North Michigan Avenue.

And do you know what happened? Nothing. Not a single Chicagoan changed a word. We still say "the Hancock." We still say "Big John." Because a name on a lease is a transaction — but a name in a city's heart is something else entirely. It is earned. As I tell my clients, your network is your net worth, and the same is true of buildings: the ones that matter are the ones people refuse to stop talking about.

A new chapter, a thousand feet up

The tower is being rewritten right now, and it is thrilling to watch. In September 2023, the legendary Signature Room on the 95th floor — where generations of Chicagoans got engaged, celebrated, and said goodbye to the year over the lights of the lake — closed its doors after thirty years.

But the sky does not stay empty for long. In June 2024, the team behind the 360 Chicago observatory purchased the 95th and 96th floors and launched the most significant interior transformation in the building's history: a new multi-story observation experience, a grand staircase between floors, all-new floor-to-ceiling glass, and a reimagining led by some of the same creative minds behind the 2024 Paris Olympics opening ceremony. The new spaces are expected to open to the public around 2027.

Big John is doing what every great Chicago property eventually does. It is reinventing itself without losing the soul that made it iconic in the first place.

"The buildings that endure aren't the ones that never change. They're the ones that change while keeping their story intact."

Why this matters to anyone buying in Chicago

I did not write this as an architecture lesson. I wrote it because the John Hancock Center is the single best argument I have for why I do what I do.

It proves that a home can be more than square footage and a view — it can be a place that means something, that holds a story worth telling, that becomes part of who you are. That is the standard I hold every property to when I represent it, whether it is a residence in the sky or a brownstone on a tree-lined street.

If you have ever stood at the base of Big John and looked up, you already understand. And if you are ready to find a home in this remarkable city that tells your story — that is exactly the conversation I live for.
