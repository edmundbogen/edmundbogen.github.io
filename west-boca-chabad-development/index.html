The Development Overview

The Chabad Chai Center (Application ZV-2023-00375) proposes a 246-seat place of worship on a 2.77-acre vacant parcel on the north side of Clint Moore Road, approximately 0.3 miles east of Wagon Wheel Drive. The site sits in unincorporated Palm Beach County, where places of worship are permitted uses by right in most zoning districts under the 2009 ULDC amendments.

The property's history shapes its current development constraints. In 2002, the northern portion of the original platted lots was acquired through eminent domain to accommodate Clint Moore Road expansion and drainage requirements. This historical taking created the geometric configuration that now requires variance requests for reduced lot size, depth, and rear setback from 100 feet to 60 feet.

The Palm Beach County Zoning Commission denied the variance in November 2023, citing the "self-imposed hardship" doctrine—noting that the current owner acquired the land after the eminent domain action and that potential lot unification with adjacent parcels owned by the same entity could resolve the dimensional constraints without requiring variances.

Traffic Analysis: A Different Pattern

Traffic concerns consistently emerge in discussions about religious facility development. However, the traffic profile of Orthodox synagogues differs fundamentally from typical houses of worship due to religious observance requirements.

Jewish law (Halakha) prohibits driving on Shabbat, meaning all congregants walk to Saturday services. This creates a complete absence of vehicular traffic during what would typically be peak demand for most houses of worship. A Dallas case study documented a 30-family Orthodox congregation with only "5-6 cars at any given time" because Shabbat attendance arrives entirely on foot.

Current traffic on Clint Moore Road ranges from 16,971 to 32,385 average annual daily trips depending on segment, with peak-hour volumes of approximately 3,017 vehicles between Jog Road and Military Trail. A facility generating minimal Saturday traffic and modest weekday attendance would represent a negligible addition to these volumes.

The primary traffic considerations involve weekday school programs and lifecycle events, which can be addressed through standard municipal coordination: dedicated turn lanes, staggered scheduling, and event-specific traffic management plans. Palm Beach County routinely conditions approvals on such mitigations.

What Academic Research Shows About Property Values

Academic studies consistently find that religious institutions have neutral to slightly positive effects on surrounding property values—contradicting assumptions that might otherwise drive neighborhood opposition.

The most rigorous U.S. study (Thompson et al., 2012, Contemporary Economic Policy) examined a newly built Mormon temple in Omaha using pre- and post-treatment hedonic pricing models. The finding: "We do not find that the religious structure influenced the value of surrounding properties."

A Hamburg study of 4,832 condominium listings found properties 109-219 yards from religious buildings listed for 4.8% more than comparable homes—an effect consistent across churches, mosques, and temples. This suggests that well-maintained religious institutions can function as neighborhood amenities rather than disamenities.

Zoning and Legal Framework

Understanding the regulatory context helps clarify what outcomes are possible. Palm Beach County zoning permits places of worship by right in most districts, with standard requirements including one parking space per three sanctuary seats, 25-foot front setbacks, and 25-30 foot maximum height.

Federal Protections

  • Religious Land Use and Institutionalized Persons Act (RLUIPA) requires equal treatment with secular assemblies
  • 11th Circuit Court affirmed protections in Gagliardi v. City of Boca Raton (2018)
  • Denials must be based on technical zoning grounds, not neighborhood opposition to religious use
  • The "without prejudice" denial signals the county is not hostile to religious use while enforcing dimensional standards

The Gagliardi precedent is instructive. When residents sued to stop a synagogue, alleging traffic and neighborhood character concerns, the court dismissed the claims—stating that such complaints are generic grievances, not constitutional injuries. This precedent effectively requires that any denial be grounded in objective technical factors such as setbacks, drainage, and parking compliance.

The Established Community Context

West Boca Raton already contains substantial Orthodox infrastructure. This is not a novel institutional type entering the area but rather an expansion of existing community patterns.

The broader Boca Raton area contains an estimated 65,000-70,000 Jewish residents, representing approximately 30-40% of the population. Existing Orthodox infrastructure includes Boca Raton Synagogue (800+ families, the largest Orthodox congregation in the Southeast U.S.), Chabad of West Boca Raton (founded 1989, now serving 600+ community members), multiple Jewish day schools with over 1,000 students, and comprehensive kosher food infrastructure with over 40 establishments.

Rabbi Zalman Bukiet's testimony captures the area's transformation: "When we first moved here, we had to go into Miami Beach to buy kosher milk. Now there is a separate kosher marketplace; the Winn-Dixie supermarket has a kosher bakery and deli; and there are five or six kosher restaurants."

A Note on Fair Housing Compliance

As a licensed real estate professional, I am obligated to note that religion is explicitly protected under the federal Fair Housing Act (42 U.S.C. §§ 3601-3631) and Florida Statutes 760.20-760.37. This legal framework prohibits:

Fair Housing Requirements

  • Making property decisions based on religious composition of neighborhoods
  • Steering buyers toward or away from areas based on religious demographics
  • Providing different service levels based on religious affiliation
  • Discussing religious factors to influence real estate decisions

Florida law specifically states: "It is unlawful to discriminate in land use decisions or in the permitting of development based on...religion" (§760.26). Any property analysis must focus exclusively on objective factors—traffic, zoning, infrastructure—rather than speculation about how religious demographics might influence real estate markets. All steering is illegal, even when well-intentioned.

This is why my analysis has focused entirely on land use data, traffic engineering, academic research on property values, and zoning compliance. These are the factors that can ethically and legally inform real estate decisions.
