Why Club Life May Add Years to Your Life

The surprising longevity benefits of golf, tennis, friendship, and showing up.

We spend fortunes on longevity. The supplements, the trainers, the concierge physicians, the wearables tracking every heartbeat. Yet one of the most powerful longevity interventions ever measured costs nothing, requires no prescription, and for many residents of Boca Raton's club communities, is already woven into daily life: other people.

The evidence is now overwhelming. In the largest analysis of its kind — 148 studies and more than 300,000 people — researchers found that individuals with strong social relationships had a 50% higher likelihood of survival over the study periods than those who were more isolated. For the richest measures of social integration, the survival advantage climbed toward 91%. To put that in perspective, the protective effect rivals quitting smoking and exceeds the benefit of most things we obsess over at the doctor's office.

In 2023, the U.S. Surgeon General took the unusual step of issuing a formal advisory on loneliness, warning that chronic social isolation carries a mortality risk comparable to smoking roughly 15 cigarettes a day. Two years later, a World Health Organization commission put a number on the global toll: social disconnection is now linked to an estimated 871,000 deaths a year — roughly one hundred lives every hour.

The protective effect of strong relationships rivals quitting smoking — and most of us are walking past it every day.

Busy Is Not the Same as Connected

Here is where the research gets interesting for our community — and where it issues a quiet warning. A packed calendar is not the same thing as a rich social life.

The data is clear that frequency of contact, on its own, barely moves the needle. You can attend every event, play in every scramble, and sit at every dinner and still register as lonely if those interactions stay on the surface. What actually predicts longevity is two things: depth and variety.

On depth: a small circle of trusted confidants — people with whom you share worries, not just small talk — is one of the most reliably protective factors science has found. The flip side is sobering. One study found that negative relationship quality, particularly a critical spouse or partner, raised five-year mortality risk by about 20%. The absence of friction may matter as much as the presence of warmth.

On variety: a 2024 study following more than 5,000 older adults found that engaging in a diversity of social roles — a club, a volunteer post, a faith community, a mentoring relationship — was associated with a 22% lower risk of death, independent of how often people socialized. And the effect grew stronger with age. It isn't the number of interactions. It's the number of different ways you belong.

It isn't how often you socialize. It's how many different ways you belong.

The Club as Longevity Infrastructure

Sociologists have a name for the spaces that aren't home and aren't work, where community is built through repeated, unforced contact: the "third place." For generations these were the corner café and the neighborhood pub. In South Florida, the country club has quietly become one of the most effective third places in American life — a single setting that delivers depth and variety on the same campus.

Consider what longevity researchers prescribe, and then consider a club calendar. The Okinawan tradition of the moai — a lifelong circle of friends who show up for one another — has a natural analog in the standing foursome and the bridge group. The "right tribe" that Blue Zones research credits for long life is, functionally, a membership of people who reinforce healthy habits. The variety of roles that the data rewards — committees, charitable boards, learning groups, intergenerational events — is precisely what a well-run club programs week after week.

And then there is the golf itself. A landmark study of nearly 300,000 golfers found a death rate roughly 40% lower than non-golfers of the same age, sex, and economic standing — an effect the researchers estimated at about five additional years of life. The relationship is associative, not proven cause-and-effect — golfers tend to be healthier to begin with — but the combination of gentle movement, time outdoors, and standing social ties maps almost perfectly onto everything else we know about healthy aging.

This is not a coincidence of luxury. It is, in effect, longevity infrastructure that residents bought into without necessarily realizing what they were buying.

Why Where You Live Is a Health Decision

For those of us who advise families on where to live, this reframes the entire conversation. A home is shelter and an asset. But a community — the kind with a clubhouse, a calendar, and a culture of showing up — is something closer to a health intervention.

It is worth being honest about the limits. Most of this evidence is observational; affluent, well-connected people also tend to have better healthcare, less financial stress, and longer baseline life expectancy, and untangling cause from correlation is genuinely hard. Palm Beach County already enjoys a life expectancy above the national average. The point is not that a membership guarantees a longer life. The point is that the ingredients science keeps pointing to — trusted friendships, varied belonging, daily movement, a reason to leave the house — are unusually concentrated in the way these communities are built.

So the takeaway is both simple and strategic. Use what you have. Trade three surface conversations for one real one. Say yes to the committee, the new group, the mentoring ask — the variety is the medicine. And when you think about your next move, weigh the social fabric of a community as seriously as you weigh the square footage. It may be the most valuable amenity on the property.

A home is an asset. A community is a health decision.

  • Strong social ties are associated with a 50% higher survival rate; the richest measures of social integration approach 91%.
  • The Surgeon General likens chronic isolation to smoking ~15 cigarettes a day; the WHO links disconnection to 871,000 deaths a year.
