What “Off-Market” Really Means in Boca Raton Luxury Real Estate

By Edmund Bogen · Douglas Elliman Real Estate · July 9, 2026

In communities like ours, “off-market” may be the most misunderstood phrase in real estate. It doesn’t mean secret. It doesn’t mean rule-free. Done properly, it means one thing: a documented process the seller directs — in writing.

Buyers often hear “off-market” and picture a hidden marketplace of homes only certain agents can unlock. Sellers sometimes imagine the opposite — a quiet handshake sale with no paperwork, no exposure, and no rules. Both pictures are wrong, and in 2026 — with private-listing practices under a national microscope, from federal litigation to portal bans — the gap between the myth and the rules has never mattered more.

Florida’s own Realtor association puts it plainly in its member guidance: off-MLS listings are not illegal or unethical when the seller directs them — but they carry additional complications. This week I want to walk through those complications honestly, because understanding them is exactly what separates a discreet, well-run private sale from an expensive mistake.

Off-Market Is a Spectrum, Not a Secret

Since the National Association of Realtors adopted its “Multiple Listing Options for Sellers” policy in March 2025, every seller in our market chooses from a menu of marketing paths — each one legitimate, each one documented, and each one governed by our local MLS’s rules. As of July 2026, in plain English:

PathWhat It Actually Means
Full MLS ListingMaximum exposure from day one — the MLS, every cooperating brokerage, and the public portals. The default, and for most sellers the path most likely to maximize price.
Coming SoonThe listing is in the MLS with a go-active date up to 21 days out. Marketing is permitted; showings wait. A structured pre-launch — not a private sale.
Delayed MarketingThe listing is filed with the MLS and visible to every cooperating agent, but held back from public portals for up to 21 days. Showings are allowed. Privacy on the public internet; full access for serious, represented buyers.
Office ExclusiveThe most private path. The listing is filed with the MLS but not disseminated, and there is no public marketing of any kind. It may be shared only within the listing brokerage and one-to-one with clients.

Notice what every path on that menu has in common: a signed listing agreement, a written seller decision, and a rulebook. The private options additionally require the seller to sign a certification acknowledging, in writing, the marketing benefits they are choosing to delay or waive. That signature isn’t bureaucracy — it’s the entire difference between a seller-directed strategy and a corner being cut.

Why Some Sellers Choose Privacy — and What It Costs

The reasons are real, and in a club community they’re familiar: no open houses, no interior photographs circulating online, fewer showings, no speculation at dinner about your asking price, and no public days-on-market clock ticking while you make unhurried decisions around a life event you’d rather keep private. For some sellers, in some seasons of life, those considerations legitimately outweigh everything else.

But here is the honest part of the conversation, and it belongs in writing before any decision is made: limited exposure is a trade-off. Fewer eyes on a property can mean fewer offers, a longer timeline, or a lower price than full exposure would produce. The best-known research points that way — a Bright MLS/Drexel University study of more than one million transactions found homes sold on the MLS commanded roughly 17.5% more than comparable off-MLS sales, and Zillow’s research puts the typical off-MLS discount around 1.5%. One independent 2026 academic study found the opposite in certain luxury tiers — so the evidence is genuinely contested — but no responsible advisor should promise that private means premium. There’s a practical wrinkle, too: under the major portals’ current standards, a home publicly marketed before it enters the MLS can lose its eligibility to appear on those portals at all. Every seller weighing a private path deserves to know all of this up front — and under MLS policy, acknowledges it in writing.

That’s why my default recommendation remains the one the data supports: the MLS is the most powerful price-discovery tool we have. Privacy is a legitimate choice — when it’s the seller’s informed choice, not an agent’s shortcut.

The Bright Line Off-market done right is a process, not a secret. And the difference between the two is a signature — the seller’s.

The Rules That Keep It Honest

A few guardrails govern all of this, and they protect you whether you’re buying or selling. Under the MLS Clear Cooperation Policy, once a property is marketed to the public — a yard sign, a social post, an email blast — it must be submitted to the MLS within one business day. And “public marketing” explicitly includes email newsletters like this one, which is why you will never see a specific private property teased in these pages: a truly private listing that appeared here would, by rule, stop being private the moment I hit send.

Florida law adds its own layer: real estate advertising must identify the brokerage, and it may never be misleading — which is why claims of an inventory of homes the public never sees should be a red flag no matter who makes them. An agent may never market a property without the owner’s written authority. And fair housing law applies to every path on the menu, public or private.

On that last point, one sentence worth being unmistakably clear about: limited exposure changes how a home is marketed — never who is welcome to buy it. Every opportunity we handle is available to any qualified buyer, consistent with the Fair Housing Act. Privacy protects a seller’s experience; it is never a filter on people.

What This Means for Buyers

Serious buyers sometimes ask how to be “in the room” when a private opportunity arises. The compliant answer is simpler than the mythology: be known, be specific, and be ready. Like most major brokerages, Douglas Elliman follows the MLS rules that govern how and when properties may be marketed. When a seller I represent has instructed us in writing to use a limited-exposure approach permitted by those rules, I share that opportunity only in the manner the seller has authorized — individually, one conversation at a time. Buyers who have registered their search criteria and are working with me as clients are considered for any such opportunity on the same terms as anyone else who inquires. There is no separate inventory, and no property is ever marketed without the seller’s written authorization.

What actually gives a buyer an edge is unglamorous: a clear written profile of what you’re looking for, proof of funds or financing in hand, and an advisor close enough to the communities you care about to recognize a genuine fit when a seller authorizes one. That’s not a velvet rope. It’s preparation.

Five questions to ask any agent pitching you an off-market sale
  1. Will my listing be filed with the MLS, as the rules require — and in which status?
  2. Will every cooperating agent be able to see it and show it to their qualified buyers?
  3. What am I giving up in portal exposure, and could pre-MLS marketing cost my home its place on those portals?
  4. Where is the written disclosure of the marketing benefits I’m waiving — and may I read it before I sign?
  5. Are you also representing a buyer for my home — and in what capacity?

An agent running a compliant process will welcome all five. Evasive answers to any of them tell you everything you need to know.

The Bottom Line

Off-market is neither a magic door nor a loophole. It’s a narrow, well-marked lane — one that exists for sellers with a genuine reason to value privacy, and that works only when the paperwork, the MLS rules, and the fair housing laws are respected to the letter. In a year when regulators, courts, and portals are all watching this corner of the industry closely, the discipline isn’t a burden. It’s the point.

If you’re weighing a discreet sale, the first step isn’t a marketing plan — it’s a confidential conversation about your goals, your timeline, and what each path on the menu would honestly mean for your outcome. And if you’re a buyer who wants to be considered when a private opportunity matching your criteria arises, the first step is telling me precisely what you’re looking for.
