Palm Beach County Is Becoming America's Private Bank

By Edmund Bogen · Douglas Elliman Real Estate · July 16, 2026

The most valuable import arriving in Palm Beach County is not people. It is balance sheets.

We talk about migration as bodies moving south — the U-Hauls, the license plates, the moving vans on I-95. But the number that actually explains what is happening to this market is not a headcount. It is an income statement. Between 2019 and 2023, Palm Beach County recorded a net inflow of $22.7 billion in adjusted gross income from Americans changing their tax address — the largest of any county in the United States, according to the MIAMI Association of Realtors' analysis of IRS migration data. Florida as a whole gained $137 billion over that stretch. Manhattan alone lost more than $40 billion.

Here is the detail that should stop you. In 2023, slightly more tax filers actually moved out of Palm Beach County than moved in — 40,922 out, 39,704 in. And the county still netted $3.04 billion in new income that year. How? The households arriving reported an average income of $178,085. The households leaving averaged $98,527. We are not simply adding residents. We are absorbing capital. Palm Beach County has become, functionally, America's private bank — and the deposits are visible everywhere you look.

Philanthropy: New Money Becomes Local Money

Wealth that relocates does not sit still — it institutionalizes. The Community Foundation for Palm Beach and Martin Counties distributed nearly $29 million in grants and scholarships in 2025, its most generous year on record, much of it flowing through donor-advised funds — precisely the vehicle transplants from high-tax states prefer. In November 2024, Baptist Health Boca Raton Regional Hospital received a $32 million gift from the Marcus Foundation, the largest in the hospital's history, anchoring a $124 million Marcus Neuroscience Institute expansion. The hospital's "Keeping the Promise" campaign has surpassed $250 million. When high-income households change their address, they do not just buy homes. They reshape hospitals, universities, and the entire civic balance sheet.

Restaurants: Manhattan Appetites, Boca Addresses

The Michelin Guide extended its Florida coverage to the Palm Beaches in 2025, and by the 2026 statewide ceremony the county had its first star: Emelina, a 16-seat Cuban tasting-menu room in West Palm Beach — the first Cuban restaurant in the world to earn one. Thomas Keller is reviving Ta-boo on Worth Avenue. Restaurant Row in Boca has thickened with concepts that would have felt out of place a decade ago and now run on waitlists. The old coat-and-tie, play-it-safe Palm Beach dining scene is gone, replaced by an audience that dines like it never left Manhattan.

Private Schools: Demand Off the Charts

The National Association of Independent Schools described enrollment growth in this region as "off the charts." Palm Beach County private-school enrollment climbed from roughly 27,700 students in 2019–20 to nearly 29,700 in 2023–24, and in Boca Raton about 26% of K-12 students now attend private school — nearly double the Florida average. Top-tier tuition runs $32,000 to $47,000. For families arriving from the Northeast, that reads as a discount against the $60,000 they were paying up north — which is exactly why the waitlists keep growing.

Private Clubs: Access Has Become a Capital Good

Initiation fees are the cleanest scarcity signal in this market. At St. Andrews Country Club, full membership initiation is now $450,000, non-refundable — plus a $50,000 contribution to the Property Owners Association at closing — and the club has just announced that in September the initiation fee rises again, to $500,000. That is up from roughly $200,000 only a few years ago. That is not an outlier: many of South Florida's top clubs have doubled or tripled their fees since the pandemic, and even the ultra-wealthy are sitting on waitlists. A club membership here is no longer a lifestyle purchase. It is relationship infrastructure — and it is priced like the asset it has become.

Professional Services: Wall Street South Goes Institutional

The migration stopped being anecdotal and became institutional in January 2026, when Wells Fargo announced it would move its wealth-management headquarters — a division generating roughly $16 billion in annual revenue — to One Flagler in West Palm Beach, the first major U.S. bank to base that business in Florida. West Palm Beach became the first office market in America to fully recover to pre-pandemic utilization, with 2025 leasing hitting a multi-year high, and the Business Development Board counts more than 300 finance firms operating in the county. Wealth does not travel alone. It brings its lawyers, accountants, and bankers with it.

Housing: A Two-Speed Market

This is where the story lands on your doorstep. Boca Raton's single-family median price hit $995,000 in the first quarter of 2025 — a record, and more than double the level of five years earlier, per Douglas Elliman's Miller Samuel report. The city's all-time record sale — $75 million in Royal Palm Yacht & Country Club — closed in May of this year, the most expensive home sold in America that month. And nearly half of all 2024 home purchases in the West Palm Beach metro were all cash — 49.6%, the highest share of any major U.S. market, against a national average of 32.6%.

But honest market intelligence requires precision: this is a two-speed market. The trophy tier keeps setting records while broader single-family and older condo segments cooled through 2025, with more inventory and longer days on market. In our own weekly tracking of 21 Boca-Delray country club communities, the spread tells the story — from a $1.89 million entry point to $19.975 million at the top of St. Andrews alone. Which side of the two-speed market you are standing on has never mattered more.

The Character Question

Boca Raton is younger, more year-round, and more institutionally networked than its snowbird stereotype — and the strains are real, too: schools over capacity, longer commutes, a workforce being priced out. Pretending otherwise would be dishonest. But the direction is unmistakable. The deposits keep arriving, and they are repricing everything they touch — homes, clubs, classrooms, and the culture itself.

The question for every owner and buyer here is no longer whether the capital is real. It is which side of it you are standing on.
