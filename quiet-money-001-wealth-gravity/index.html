Wealth Gravity

Why the world's wealth is sorting itself — and where St. Andrews fits in the map being redrawn.

By Edmund Bogen

The line being drawn

Wealth is concentrating faster than at any point in the modern era, and it is doing so along lines most people have not yet noticed. The story is no longer about how much money is being made. It is about where the money is going, who is moving with it, and which places on the map are being quietly chosen — and which are being quietly left.

Three jurisdictions on earth are absorbing a disproportionate share of the global wealth migration: the United Arab Emirates, Singapore, and the United States. Within the United States, the share is collapsing toward two states — Florida and Texas. Within Florida, it is collapsing toward two counties — Miami-Dade and Palm Beach. Within Palm Beach County, it is collapsing toward a short list of communities. St. Andrews Country Club is on that list.

The purpose of this column is not to sell anyone a house. It is to give the readers of this newsletter — most of whom are operating from inside the community I cover — a sober, useful read of the global wealth picture and what it means for the place you actually live.

The thesis for this first issue is straightforward. The forces pulling capital toward Boca Raton are global, structural, and accelerating. The forces pushing capital out of the high-friction jurisdictions you may have left — New York, California, Illinois, the United Kingdom — are not cyclical. They are policy decisions, and the people writing them are not changing their minds. What that means for the value of a home inside the St. Andrews gates over the next decade is something worth thinking about clearly, because most of your neighbors are not yet.

The tiers, redefined for 2026

The word "millionaire" no longer means anything useful. It has been hollowed out by thirty years of inflation, asset appreciation, and the fact that a paid-off home in a decent zip code now clears the threshold by itself. The tiers most serious wealth researchers — Knight Frank, UBS, Capgemini, Henley — now work from look roughly like this.

Upper Middle Class — $500K to $2M net worth. Generally one or two paid-off homes, retirement accounts, modest taxable assets. Commercial first class on long flights, occasionally. Private school is a stretch. Inflation is felt.

Affluent — $2M to $5M. A primary residence and possibly a small second home. Jet card considered, rarely used. Children's tuition manageable but conscious. Inflation is monitored.

High Net Worth — $5M to $30M. This is the entry tier for most of St. Andrews. Two or three homes. NetJets or Wheels Up fractional, or a serious jet card. A part-time house manager or estate manager. Concierge medicine. A family attorney on retainer. Estate planning in active motion. Inflation is structural rather than emotional.

Very High Net Worth — $30M to $100M. Whole aircraft becomes rational. Full household staff. A family office or shared family office. Direct private equity allocations. Children's wealth structured separately. Political donations are returned phone calls.

Ultra High Net Worth — $100M to $1B. Full single-family office. Multiple residences across multiple jurisdictions. A general counsel. A chief of staff. Personal security at events. The IRS knows your name.

Uber Wealthy — $1B and above. A different category of human existence. Influence rather than wealth. The decisions you make move markets, towns, and sometimes governments.

The most important distinction, though, is not between the tiers. It is within them — between the income rich, the asset rich, and the liquid rich. A surgeon earning $1.8M in Manhattan is income rich and frequently neither of the other two. A founder who sold for $80M is liquid rich and may or may not be asset rich. Many St. Andrews residents are profoundly asset rich — a paid-off home in this community is meaningful capital — but materially less liquid than the balance sheet suggests, particularly once you account for the illiquidity of real estate and private holdings. Meanwhile, a newer generation of arrivals — tech founders in their forties, finance principals who left New York during COVID and never went back — are extremely liquid but light on the kind of multigenerational asset base that defines old money.

In 2026, the meaningful question is no longer how much you have. It is how much of it is deployable, mobile, and protected.

Calibration

A short historical pass is worth doing, because the numbers do not feel like what they used to mean.

In 1955, $1M was an extraordinary amount of money. A Manhattan apartment on Park Avenue cost roughly $30,000. A Mercedes 300SL cost about $7,000. A four-year private college education cost under $4,000 all-in. Country club initiation fees at top-tier clubs ran in the low four figures. A millionaire was rare, untouchable, and effectively retired.

By 1985, $1M was still serious money but the gap had begun to open. A Park Avenue two-bedroom cost roughly $400,000. A St. Andrews home — when this community was still being developed — could be had for $300,000 to $500,000. Harvard tuition was around $10,000. The millionaire was comfortable, not exceptional.

By 2000, $1M was respectable but no longer remarkable. The same Park Avenue apartment cost $1.5M. The same St. Andrews home traded for $700,000 to $1.2M. Harvard tuition was $32,000.

In 2026, that 1985 dollar buys you roughly thirty-three cents of what it bought then. The same Park Avenue apartment crosses $3M without renovation. The same St. Andrews home — depending on the street and the build — now trades in a typical resale range of $1.9M to $7M. A newer category of inventory — new-build and waterfront, which barely existed a decade ago — pushes the absolute ceiling to roughly $17M in 2026, but that segment sits above the typical resale market, not within it. Harvard's all-in cost is north of $90,000. A Mercedes that competes with the 300SL costs over $200,000.

The number has not kept up with the life. A millionaire in 1985 had the purchasing power of roughly $3M today. A millionaire in 2000 had the purchasing power of about $1.85M. The word has been emptied. The category, in any meaningful lifestyle sense, now begins north of $5M.

What Knight Frank is telling us

The Knight Frank Wealth Report 2026 is the document I read first every year, and this year's edition is worth pausing on. A few numbers from it should sit with the reader.

The global UHNW population — defined as individuals with $30M or more in investable net worth — reached 713,626 in 2026, up from 551,435 in 2021. That is roughly 89 new $30M-plus households created every day for five consecutive years. The United States generated 41% of that growth. Not 41% of the new wealth in absolute dollars — 41% of the new individuals.

That number is worth reading twice. Forty-one percent of all newly minted $30M-plus households on earth, over the past five years, were created in America. The country's share of the global UHNW total climbed from 33% to 35% over the same window. China remains the second-largest UHNW population, holding a 17% global share — but its share of the total is gently shrinking, not expanding. The implication is that whatever else is happening globally, the United States is still the most efficient wealth-creation engine on the planet. Within America, the new wealth is sorting itself geographically with unusual speed.

On the property side, Knight Frank's Prime International Residential Index — which tracks 100 luxury markets globally — rose 3.2% in 2025. The Middle East as a region rose 9.4%. Dubai prime rose 25.1%. Tokyo prime — measured in local currency and concentrated in prime new-build apartments — rose 58.5%, a figure that is almost difficult to absorb in a single sitting. (Because the yen was weak through 2025, the USD-denominated return for foreign buyers was materially smaller; the headline number is the local-currency repricing, not the dollar-denominated one.) Miami appears on Knight Frank's official list of forward-looking luxury hot spots alongside Mumbai, Brisbane, and Hong Kong.

The most consequential line in the entire report, however, is not a statistic. It is a sentence from Liam Bailey, Knight Frank's Global Head of Research:

Every market that wants to succeed in attracting UHNW capital over the next decade needs to be positioned at an attractive point on the tax curve. Capital is already moving away from high-friction environments toward jurisdictions that actively court wealth. Liam Bailey, Global Head of Research, Knight Frank

Read that quote twice as well. It is the underlying argument of this entire newsletter. The world is sorting itself into jurisdictions that court wealth and jurisdictions that punish it, and capital is making its choice in real time. Forty-one percent of new global UHNW formation happened in the country with the most tax-attractive states. Within that country, those states are pulling in the disproportionate share of the migration. Miami is on Knight Frank's watchlist. Boca Raton sits 45 minutes north of Miami and is increasingly absorbing the spillover — the buyer who wants Miami's tax and climate profile without Miami's volume, exposure, and traffic.

Wealth Gravity

The shorthand I use for this is Wealth Gravity — the phenomenon by which capital clusters around jurisdictions that offer some combination of favorable taxation, physical safety, elite professional networks, airport infrastructure, deep liquidity, luxury and medical amenities, school options, privacy, and capital preservation. The places with the most of these attributes do not just attract money. They attract the people who control it, and then they attract the institutions that serve those people, and then the gravity self-reinforces.

The strong gravity wells on the current map are Florida, Texas, Dubai, Singapore, Monaco, Switzerland, the broader UAE, and to a lesser extent Portugal. The anti-gravity jurisdictions — places losing capital faster than they are creating it — are California, New York, Illinois, the United Kingdom following its non-dom reforms, and significant portions of continental Europe. France remains a complicated case. The UK case is now well-documented: the abolition of the non-dom regime has produced an exodus of HNW and UHNW individuals that the Treasury did not forecast and is now scrambling to slow.

Within the United States, the domestic story is even sharper. Florida and Texas are absorbing the bulk of the interstate wealth migration. Within Florida, Palm Beach County has been quietly outperforming Miami-Dade on a per-capita basis for several years. Within Palm Beach County, Boca Raton occupies a specific position: it offers the tax and climate profile of Miami, the proximity to Palm Beach itself, three private aviation terminals within a thirty-minute drive, two of the most highly regarded hospital systems in the Southeast, and a country-club density unmatched anywhere in the state.

And within Boca Raton, St. Andrews occupies its own position. A 24/7 gated, guard-staffed community. A buyer pool that increasingly includes 40-something tech and finance principals who, a decade ago, would have bought in Greenwich or Pacific Heights. Inventory that turns more slowly than the broader market, on smaller volume, at higher absolute price points.

Where wealth is quietly disappearing

This is the section most newsletters skip, and it is the section that should be read most carefully — because many of you have adult children or grandchildren living inside what I am about to describe.

The professional and upper-professional class is being compressed. Not the top one percent. The top ten percent. The household earning $300,000 to $500,000 in New York City, San Francisco, Boston, or Los Angeles no longer feels wealthy, and in any honest balance-sheet accounting, increasingly is not. After federal, state, and city taxes; after a $5,000-per-month rent or a mortgage on a $2.5M condo; after $80,000 in two kids' private school tuition; after concierge medicine and a second car and travel and saving for college and saving for retirement — there is very little left, and the asset base is not compounding fast enough to outrun the cost floor.

Healthcare and education inflation continue to outpace wage growth for the top decile. The top one percent absorbs this easily. The top ten percent does not.

AI is accelerating the squeeze. The functions that historically funneled bright young professionals into the affluent tier — legal associates, junior bankers, management consultants, paralegals, copywriters, much of mid-tier advisory work — are being compressed faster than most senior executives in those industries are willing to admit publicly. The income streams that built the parents' wealth are not the same income streams that will be available to the children.

The distinction between appearing wealthy and being wealthy has never been wider. A lifestyle that looked indistinguishable from real wealth in 2005 can now be sustained by a household with a deeply negative net worth, and frequently is. The smart reader of this column will see his or her own children's financial life somewhere in this paragraph, and will think about what that means for the family balance sheet over the next twenty years.

The psychology of modern wealth

A few quieter shifts are worth noting, because they describe how the affluent in 2026 actually behave — and the description tends to be uncomfortably accurate.

Logo wealth has been replaced by stealth wealth. The signal goods of the moment are Loro Piana, Brunello Cucinelli, an unbranded vintage Patek, an older Range Rover, a renovation done by an architect the public has never heard of. The person wearing the obvious logo is, increasingly, not the person you think you are looking at.

Privacy and security have moved from luxury to baseline. Gated communities, residential security details, scrubbed public records, LLC ownership of homes, separate phone numbers for the household, separate calendars for the children — these are no longer paranoid. They are operational.

Concierge medicine — Sollis, MDVIP, One Medical, Forward, the major hospital-affiliated concierge programs — has become standard rather than aspirational at the HNW tier. Longevity protocols have moved from fringe to mainstream within five years. The Bryan Johnson reference has stopped being a joke.

Optionality is the new luxury. The ability to leave any city within 48 hours, to hold residency in two or three jurisdictions, to move capital between currencies without friction, to move children between schools — this is what the wealthy are actually buying now. Not bigger houses. More exit doors.

And time has become the only truly scarce resource. Anyone reading this is older than they were when they first earned real money, and the calculus has shifted accordingly.

What this means for prime real estate

Every thread above lands here.

Prime residential real estate remains the dominant non-financial wealth-storage mechanism on earth, for a specific set of reasons. It is tangible. It is leverageable. It is depreciable under certain structures. It is inflation-hedged in a way few other assets are. It transfers across generations with reasonable efficiency. And it carries a use-value the rest of the portfolio does not: the family actually lives in it.

A few specific patterns are now visible in the data.

Branded residences — Aman, Four Seasons, Ritz, Rosewood, Bulgari, Auberge — are growing globally at double-digit rates and pulling premium pricing the unbranded inventory cannot match. South Florida has been a primary beneficiary.

Private country club communities are quietly having a renaissance after a decade of obituaries. The drivers are exactly what they should be: security, social network density, intergenerational continuity, and a club amenity that the broader market cannot replicate. St. Andrews is in the deepest version of this trend, not at the edge of it.

Buyers at the prime end are now underwriting for resilience — climate, political, infrastructure — in a way they were not five years ago. Florida fits well on several axes and is vulnerable on others. Insurance markets remain a real conversation. Hurricane exposure is real. Long-term water and grid resilience is real. None of these are deal-killers at this end of the market; they are inputs to the purchase decision.

And the great intergenerational wealth transfer — Cerulli's most recent estimate is $124 trillion changing hands through 2048 in the United States alone, of which roughly $105 trillion moves to heirs and $18 trillion to charity — is now meaningfully underway. South Florida sits on the receiving end of a disproportionate share of that handoff. The capital is not arriving in 2040. It is arriving now.

One specific observation about St. Andrews, drawn from what is actually closing here: the buyer pool today is materially different from the buyer pool of even five years ago. Younger. More liquid. More tech and finance, less traditional industry. More likely to maintain a primary residence in another state or country. More demanding on renovation and finish standards. This is not forecast. It is data.

What it means going forward

The next decade is not going to produce a more equal distribution of wealth. It is going to produce greater concentration at the top, sharper geographic sorting of the affluent and the wealthy, wider gaps between asset owners and non-owners, more mobile capital, more competitive jurisdictions, and more sophisticated buyers at the prime end of every meaningful market.

The people reading this newsletter already understand, in some quiet way, what the report writers are now beginning to formalize: real wealth in 2026 is measured less in dollars than in freedom, time, health, security, optionality, and the quality of the community around you. That is what St. Andrews is. It is also, increasingly, what the rest of the world is paying for. The prime end of this market is unlikely to soften meaningfully over the next decade, even when the broader market does, because the people moving capital toward it are not making cyclical decisions. They are making structural ones.

The map is being redrawn quietly, in private. This column will keep reading it out loud.

— Edmund Bogen
