Pocket Listings at St. Andrews Country Club
An Owner's Guide for 2026

A respectful, general-information primer on how pocket listings work inside our community, what current research shows, and how to think about the conversation if a sale is on your mind.

Owners are curious about what it actually means, how it works inside a private club like ours, and whether it's the right choice when the time comes to think about selling. What A Pocket Listing Actually Is

"Pocket listing" is an industry term for a home offered for sale with intentionally limited public exposure. No yard sign. No MLS distribution. No public marketing. The home is shown to a small, hand-selected group of buyers, often through the listing agent's personal network.

In 2026, the more precise term used by the National Association of REALTORS® is "office exclusive." Under NAR's Clear Cooperation Policy — effective January 1, 2020, and refined again on March 25, 2025, under a framework called Multiple Listing Options for Sellers — there are now two clearly defined exempt categories:

  • Office Exclusive — the seller instructs the broker not to publicly market the property and not to disseminate it through the MLS. A signed seller disclosure is required.
  • Delayed Marketing — the listing is filed with the MLS, but public IDX and syndication exposure is delayed for a period the local MLS allows. A signed seller disclosure is also required.

Both exist for legitimate reasons. Both require informed written consent from the seller acknowledging that the broader benefits of MLS-wide exposure are being temporarily set aside.

The general rule under Clear Cooperation is straightforward: once a listing is marketed publicly — through a sign, a brokerage website, an email blast, or a multi-brokerage app — it must be submitted to the MLS within one business day. That clarifies what was once a gray area.

In Florida, there is no statewide statutory ban on pocket listings. The operative rules are the local MLS rules and the listing broker's fiduciary duty to the seller. St. Andrews properties fall under Beaches MLS, so any pocket listing inside the gates must follow Beaches MLS exempt-listing procedures along with Clear Cooperation timing.

This is general information about how pocket listings work. It is not legal advice — listing agreements, exempt-listing disclosures, and agency rules are situation-specific and should be reviewed with your attorney.

Why Pocket Listings Exist Inside Private Clubs

There are real, legitimate reasons sellers in private-club communities sometimes prefer a quieter sale.

Privacy. For families with public visibility, financial discretion concerns, or sensitive personal circumstances — a divorce, an estate transition, a health-driven move — keeping the sale out of public view matters. That's a perfectly valid preference.

Controlled exposure. Some sellers prefer not to have a parade of unqualified showings through their home. A pocket listing allows the agent to filter for buyers who already understand the community, the membership commitment, and the price range.

Quiet pre-marketing. Sometimes a seller wants time to finish updates, complete photography, or prepare the property properly before launch. A short period of quiet pre-marketing can serve that goal.

A natural fit for tight communities. In a community like ours — 740 custom estate homes on 720 beautifully maintained acres, with mandatory membership and a structured buyer pool — agents who serve the club regularly know which buyers have been waiting, what they want, and when to make the introduction. That kind of careful matchmaking has value.

Pocket listings are a tool used by professional agents for legitimate reasons. They are part of how a private-club market like ours operates.

What The Research Shows About Outcomes

It's also worth being honest about what the published research says, so owners can make an informed decision.

Bright MLS, working with researchers at Drexel University, studied on-MLS sales versus off-MLS sales from 2019 through early 2023. Their published finding is that on-MLS sales transacted for approximately 17.5% more than comparable off-MLS sales on average. That figure is widely cited in industry trade press and represents a meaningful gap in average outcomes.

A separate Bright MLS study of office exclusives reported two additional findings:

  • Nearly two-thirds of office exclusives eventually ended up on the MLS anyway. The quiet sale didn't conclude, and the property ultimately needed broader exposure.
  • Properties that began as office exclusives generally took longer to sell than properties listed openly from the start.

These are averages across many markets and many price points. They don't dictate the right answer for any single seller. But they do mean the conversation about a pocket listing should be a real conversation — weighing privacy, timing, and circumstance on one side against statistical exposure benefits on the other.

These are published research findings shared as general information. Past results are not a guarantee of future outcomes in any specific transaction.

Why 2026 Is Worth Paying Attention To Inside St. Andrews

A few things are happening in the broader market and inside the community that are worth understanding if you're an owner or a prospective buyer here.

The market inside the gates is moving briskly. Per published market data sources, average days on market for St. Andrews homes have come down meaningfully year-over-year — recent figures show properties moving in roughly half the time they were a year ago. Median sale prices in St. Andrews have shown double-digit year-over-year growth as of late 2025, and the high end of the community continues to set new benchmarks per square foot. Different data providers report somewhat different figures depending on segment and timeframe; current MLS data should always be the reference point for any specific decision.

Inventory remains structurally tight. With roughly 740 estate homes and only a small number publicly listed at any given moment, supply represents a very small percentage of the community on any given day. Qualified buyers who want into St. Andrews often wait patiently for the right opportunity to surface.

The membership cost of entry continues to evolve. As of 2026, the total cost due at closing for new St. Andrews membership is approximately $500,000, which includes a $50,000 POA owner's capital contribution. That financial commitment naturally produces a buyer pool that is serious, well-prepared, and committed to the community for the long term.

The broader South Florida private-club market is investing heavily in its future. Across the region, peer clubs are executing significant capital programs — investments that speak to the underlying strength and confidence in the South Florida luxury private-club lifestyle as a whole. That rising tide of regional investment is good news for everyone who owns inside any of these communities.

The St. Andrews community itself continues to invest in and evaluate its long-term direction. Member engagement around the club's future is active and thoughtful. Owners who take pride in this community know that its leadership and membership care deeply about what it should be a decade from now. That ongoing dialogue is part of what keeps St. Andrews distinctive.

What All Of This Means For An Owner Considering A Sale

If you are an owner at St. Andrews quietly weighing whether and how to bring your home to market, here is a balanced way to think about it:

A pocket listing remains a legitimate option when privacy, timing, or specific circumstances make it the right call. Used appropriately, with informed consent and proper compliance, it is a tool that experienced agents in this community use effectively.

An open MLS listing offers measurable exposure benefits that, on average, correlate with stronger sale outcomes — particularly in markets where qualified buyer demand is healthy, as it is in St. Andrews this year.

The right answer depends on you — your privacy needs, your timeline, your situation, and your goals. There is no universal right path, and any good agent will walk you through both options honestly before recommending one.

The conversation worth having in 2026 is not "should I do a pocket listing or list publicly." It's "given my specific situation and the current market, what approach best serves my goals?" That conversation deserves real care.
