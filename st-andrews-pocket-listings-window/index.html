What People Mean When They Say "Pocket Listing"

A pocket listing is industry shorthand for a property offered for sale with intentionally limited public exposure. No yard sign. No MLS distribution. No Zillow. A handful of agents whisper to a handful of buyers. Sometimes a deal happens. Sometimes the home sits in the pocket until the seller and the agent agree to bring it into the light.

In 2026, the cleaner term is "office exclusive." That language comes from the National Association of REALTORS®, which adopted a national rule called the Clear Cooperation Policy effective January 1, 2020 — and updated it again in March 2025 with a framework called Multiple Listing Options for Sellers.

The short version of the rule: once a listing is marketed publicly, it has to be submitted to the MLS within one business day. Yard signs count. Brokerage websites count. Email blasts count. Multi-brokerage sharing apps count. Going public is now a defined moment, not a gray area.

There are two legitimate exempt categories under the 2025 framework:

  • Office Exclusive — the seller directs the broker not to publicly market and not to disseminate through the MLS. Signed seller disclosure required.
  • Delayed Marketing — listed in the MLS, but public IDX and syndication exposure is delayed for a period the local MLS allows. Signed seller disclosure required.

Both require a signed seller acknowledgment that they understand the benefits of MLS-wide exposure are being waived.

In Florida, there is no statewide statutory ban on pocket listings. The operative rules are local MLS rules and the listing broker's fiduciary duty to the seller. St. Andrews falls under Beaches MLS, so a property listed inside the gates that wants to stay quiet has to follow Beaches MLS exempt-listing procedures and Clear Cooperation timing.

This is general information about how pocket listings work. It is not legal advice. Listing agreements, exempt-listing disclosures, and state agency law are situation-specific — please consult your attorney for any specific transaction question.

The Pocket Listing Inside A Private Club

Here is why pocket listings have a home in places like St. Andrews.

The community has 740 custom estate homes on 720 acres. At any moment, eight to thirteen properties are publicly listed. Do the math: roughly 1-2% of the community is on the market on any given day. Inventory is structurally low. The buyers who want in know it.

Add the financial bar to enter: a non-refundable initiation fee of approximately $400,000, plus a $25,000 POA owner's capital contribution at closing, on top of the home itself. That is a serious threshold. Buyers who clear it tend to know exactly what they want. They wait. Sometimes they wait a long time.

That waiting creates a private channel. Agents who farm the club hear about a sale before the listing goes live. A few buyers get a phone call. Occasionally a deal happens before the yard sign would have gone up.

Pocket listings are not a scandal. They are a tool. But the data on whether they serve sellers — particularly in seller's markets — has gotten harder to ignore.

What The Research Says

Bright MLS, in partnership with researchers at Drexel University, studied on-MLS sales versus off-MLS sales from 2019 through early 2023. Their published finding: on-MLS sales transacted for 17.5% more than comparable off-MLS sales. That is a publicly cited figure across industry trade press and a significant gap.

A separate Bright MLS office-exclusive study found two more facts worth knowing:

  • Nearly two-thirds of office exclusives eventually end up on the MLS anyway. The quiet sale didn't conclude. The market needed to see the property.
  • Properties that started as office exclusives generally took longer to sell than properties listed openly from day one.

None of this means pocket listings are wrong for every seller. Privacy matters. Timing matters. There are legitimate reasons — divorce, estate transitions, public-figure exposure, prep work on the property — to keep a sale quiet for a window. But the data has shifted the conversation. Privacy is a real benefit. It is also a real cost.

So What Is Actually Changing In St. Andrews

This is the part of the story that matters most.

The clubhouse is done. St. Andrews completed a $27.5 million Club Enhancement Plan in late 2023 — a full upgrade of the 125,000-square-foot clubhouse, dining venues, aquatic center, and recreation facilities. The amenity refresh has been digested into current pricing. Buyers walking the property today are seeing the finished product.

Days on market have collapsed. Per published market data, average days on market in St. Andrews dropped from approximately 132 days in the prior period to roughly 53 days in the most recent period. That is a 60% reduction. Velocity is up.

Prices are moving up. Published market sources report median sale prices in St. Andrews up approximately 12% year-over-year as of late 2025, with trailing-twelve-month averages for premium properties cited in the $4M+ range and per-square-foot figures in the $670+ range. Different data providers report slightly different figures depending on segment and timeframe — verify with current MLS data for any specific transaction.

Peer clubs are in the middle of their renovations, not after. Look at the field around St. Andrews:

South Florida Peer-Club Capital Programs

  • Boca West Country Club — executing a $95 million capital improvement plan. Course 1 broke ground in March 2026. The pickleball center is targeting May 2026 completion. Members are living through construction.
  • Mirasol Country Club — mid-execution on an $82 million Phase 2 master plan, with new multipurpose buildings, indoor pickleball, and course restorations underway.
  • The Polo Club of Boca Raton — in a three-year clubhouse transformation running through mid-2027.
  • Broken Sound Club — completed a $40 million clubhouse renovation in 2023; finished and absorbed.
  • Frenchman's Creek Beach & Country Club — opened a new $74 million, 124,450-square-foot clubhouse in late 2024, raising initiation to approximately $475,000 plus a $25,000 POA capital contribution effective March 1, 2026.
  • Addison Reserve Country Club — completed a $24 million Rees Jones golf renovation and raised full-golf initiation from $325,000 to $400,000 effective January 1, 2026.

Two things are happening simultaneously. First, the St. Andrews fee structure that looked aggressive two years ago now sits comfortably inside the peer-club range. Frenchman's Creek crossed $475K. Addison Reserve crossed $400K. The St. Andrews $400,000 number is no longer an outlier — it is the new normal in South Florida luxury golf.

Second, the construction noise next door creates a quiet preference for St. Andrews right now. Buyers who were touring Boca West, Mirasol, or Polo and could not get past the scaffolding sometimes redirect to clubs that are already finished. St. Andrews is finished.

New supply is coming. Custom estate builds inside St. Andrews with anticipated Q2-Q3 2026 delivery dates are in the pipeline. When those close, they reset comparable prices at the high end of the per-square-foot range.

Why This Matters For Anyone Thinking About A Sale

The market reading is not a prediction. It is a description of current conditions:

  • Inventory is at structural lows.
  • Days on market are down approximately 60%.
  • Median prices are up double-digits year-over-year.
  • The amenity story inside the gates is "finished and proven," not "promised and uncertain."
  • Peer clubs are mid-renovation; some of their buyers are looking around.
  • New construction supply is on the way later in 2026.

In conditions like these, the pocket listing — the quiet sale, the off-market channel — is competing against some of the strongest open-market dynamics St. Andrews has seen in years. The published data from Bright MLS suggests on-MLS exposure produces stronger sale prices on average. The conditions inside St. Andrews right now suggest open exposure has more buyers competing for it than usual.

That does not mean every seller should list publicly. Personal circumstances vary. Privacy may matter more than the last dollar. An estate, a relocation, a divorce — there are real reasons a quiet sale is the right call. But the conversation an owner has with their REALTOR® in 2026 should include the current data, the current peer-club landscape, and an honest read of what is gained and lost on each path.

The pocket listing isn't dead. It is just no longer the default smart move it sometimes was. The market caught up.

A Final Thought On Timing

There is a saying in luxury real estate: the best window is the one where buyers are still waiting and supply is not yet flowing. Inside St. Andrews right now, buyers have been waiting through two years of peer-club construction and tight inventory. New construction supply lands later this year. That is the picture. Make of it what you will.
