The crossover nobody can ignore.

For nearly a decade, New York held a title that seemed untouchable: the largest inventory of million-dollar homes in America. That era is over. According to Realtor.com's Luxury Housing Market Trends and Outlook 2026, the Miami–Fort Lauderdale–West Palm Beach metro closed 2025 with 10,513 active million-dollar listings — decisively ahead of New York's 9,216. The December 2025 monthly snapshot told the same story: 10,591 seven-figure listings in Florida versus 10,176 in New York.

Read that metro name again. Miami–Fort Lauderdale–West Palm Beach. The market that just took New York's crown is not a single city — it is the corridor that runs straight through Palm Beach. The island and its surrounding communities are not watching this story from the sidelines. They are inside it.

What actually happened.

Let's be precise, because precision is what separates analysis from hype. Florida did not become "more expensive" than New York. New York still carries the higher luxury entry bar — by Realtor.com's measure, the price of admission to its top tier remains meaningfully above South Florida's. And New York briefly reclaimed the listing-count lead this spring, as it always surges with the April selling season.

What changed is deeper and more durable. Realtor.com's senior economist described it as a "structural shift in where luxury inventory is accumulating" — and structural is the word that matters. South Florida's luxury supply has become broader, deeper, and far less seasonal than New York's — a market that builds, lists, and sells seven-figure homes twelve months a year, while Manhattan's inventory swells in spring and drains every winter.

"Miami is not replacing New York. It is joining New York as a co-capital of American luxury real estate." — Dottie Herman, Vice Chair, Douglas Elliman

Why the capital moved south.

Three forces drove the crossover, and all three are still running.

The three forces behind the shift

The wealth migration is real and measurable. Realtor.com found that 26.3% of South Florida's luxury demand originates in the New York metro area — a larger share than the next eight feeder markets combined. This is not anecdote. More than one in four luxury buyers looking at South Florida is looking from New York.
Global capital chose Florida. The National Association of Realtors reports that foreign buyers purchased $56 billion of U.S. homes in the year ending March 2025 — up 33.2% — and Florida captured 21% of those buyers, the highest share of any state, ahead of California, Texas, and New York. Nearly half of those international purchases were all-cash.
The buyer base is built differently. South Florida's luxury market runs heavily on cash buyers, international purchasers, retirees, and second-home owners — demographics far less sensitive to mortgage rates and school calendars. That is why Florida's inventory holds steady through the winter months while New York's thins out every winter.

What this means in Palm Beach.

Here is where the headline becomes a strategy.

For sellers: more million-dollar inventory metro-wide means more competition for buyer attention. The sellers who win in this environment are the ones who price with discipline and present with precision. Aspirational pricing was forgivable in 2021. In a market with over 10,000 active seven-figure listings, it is how a home goes stale.

For buyers: depth means choice, but not uniform leverage. The gap is widening between luxury by price tag and luxury by durability — the homes with the right location, the right condition, and the right story still command scarcity pricing, while everything else negotiates. Knowing the difference is the entire game.

For homeowners watching the market: the structural forces that pushed Florida past New York — tax advantages, wealth migration, global capital — flow directly through the Palm Beach corridor. The buyers arriving from the Northeast are not all landing on Brickell. A meaningful share of them want the island, the water, and the lifestyle Palm Beach is known for — and they are arriving every season.

The migration is not a forecast. It is already on the closing sheets.

The bottom line.

The center of gravity in American luxury real estate has shifted south — not as a fad, but as a structural realignment of where wealth lives, invests, and lists. New York remains a great luxury market. But for the first time in a decade, it is no longer the deepest one. That market is ours.
