# The Summer Deadline: Why Relocating Families Are Making This the Busiest Season in Boca Raton's Club Communities

**By Edmund Bogen | Douglas Elliman Real Estate**
*Member and Resident of St. Andrews Country Club*
*July 1, 2026*

The clock that's driving Boca Raton's luxury market right now isn't interest rates. It's the first day of school.

For decades, our market ran on the snowbird calendar — buyers arrived after Thanksgiving, shopped through season, and closed by Easter. That rhythm still exists. But the buyer reshaping our club communities today runs on a completely different schedule. Dual-income tech and finance families — the portfolio manager and the physician, the fintech executive and the attorney — don't shop when the weather up north turns cold. They shop when the school calendar tells them to.

And that calendar says: now. In Palm Beach County, the 2026–2027 school year begins **August 10, 2026** — a hard, public deadline every relocating family is already working backward from. *(Source: Palm Beach County School District, 2026–2027 Key Dates.)*

## Why Summer Became the New Season

When a family with school-age children decides to relocate to South Florida, everything works backward from one immovable date — the morning their kids walk into a new classroom. Enrollment paperwork, admissions decisions, uniforms, carpool logistics, and the emotional work of helping a child land in a new community all have to happen before that date, not after.

That means the home has to close in summer. Which means the offer has to happen in early summer. Which means the search — often conducted remotely, on video tours, between board meetings — is happening right now, with an intensity the traditional winter market rarely matches.

The moving industry has quietly understood this for years: the majority of American families relocate between May and August specifically to avoid disrupting the school year, with July and August the peak crunch. *(Source: Coldwell Banker / national moving-industry data, 2025.)* This is why realtors across our club communities have been running at full speed through months that used to be quiet. It's not a fluke. It's a structural change in who's buying and why.

## The Buyer Behind the Urgency

The wealth migration story is no longer news — but its second act is. The first wave of relocation was heavy on empty-nesters and tax refugees, and they could shop whenever they pleased. The current wave is the employees and partners those firms brought with them: two-career households in prime earning and child-rearing years, following the financial firms, family offices, and technology companies that have planted flags across Palm Beach County.

The numbers behind that inflow are staggering. More than **250 financial firms** have relocated or expanded into Palm Beach County over the past decade — the current wave, beginning in 2025, is the third major migration after 2013 and 2020. *(Source: Business Development Board of Palm Beach County, "Wall Street South.")* The county now hosts **more than 380 single-family offices** and roughly 90 multi-family offices — a **45% jump in family-office formation since 2020.** *(Source: KiTalent / Palm Beach family-office hiring data, 2025.)* Names still arriving or expanding include Citadel, Point72, BlackRock, Elliott Management, Goldman Sachs, and Baron Funds, and the tech sector has followed — ServiceNow announced a West Palm Beach regional headquarters and AI hub in October 2025. *(Sources: BDB; MILLION Corporate Migration Wave 2024–2025.)*

Here's where it gets interesting: this buyer has money, motivation, and almost no flexibility on timing. A retiree can wait for the perfect house. A family that starts a new role in September and has two children who need to be enrolled by August cannot. When motivated buyers meet a hard deadline, they act decisively — and they compete.

And the schools they're competing to get into run their own clocks. Boca Raton's most sought-after private schools — Saint Andrew's School, Pine Crest, American Heritage, Grandview — build admissions timelines that reward early movers and punish late ones. Saint Andrew's regular-decision checklist closes in mid-January with decisions in February, and its "waitpool" holds qualified families with no guaranteed spot. *(Source: Saint Andrew's School Admissions.)* A family that lands here in July is often threading a needle that started months earlier.

> **St. Andrews Country Club Insight:** From my perspective inside the gates, the change is visible on any summer weekend — families touring with kids in tow, questions about camps and youth programming before questions about the golf course, and offers written days after a first showing, not weeks. The school calendar has turned our slowest months into some of our most decisive.

## The Inventory Reality

Deadline-driven demand meeting tight supply produces exactly what you'd expect. Boca Raton's luxury single-family median days on market has tightened to roughly **33 days, down from 40** a year ago, and the median luxury sale price has climbed to **$2,192,500** with price-per-square-foot up to **$648 from $546.** *(Source: Premier Estate Properties / Boca Raton Luxury Market Report 2026.)* South Florida recorded **361 sales above $10 million in 2025 — the highest total since 2021.** *(Source: MILLION Luxury.)* Well-positioned homes are moving in days: a St. Andrews estate recently closed at $12 million after just **four days on market.** *(Source: Behind The Hedges.)*

Inside our own gates, the MLS confirms the pattern. Across the 21 Boca club communities I track, inventory sits at roughly **2.6 months** — firmly a seller's market — and the fastest-moving clubs are the tightest of all: Polo Club, The Bridges, Broken Sound, and Woodfield are all turning over inventory faster than the market can replace it. When demand is deadline-driven, that scarcity doesn't just hold prices — it accelerates decisions.

## What This Means Right Now

**If you're a seller with a move-in-ready home in a club community:** The conventional wisdom says wait for season to list. This year, that wisdom may cost you. The most motivated, deadline-driven buyers of the year are in the market today, and they're paying premiums for turnkey homes they can close and occupy before school starts. A renovated home with a home office and impact glass, listed now, meets this buyer at the exact moment they must act. Waiting until December means marketing to a more leisurely — and more negotiation-minded — audience.

**If you're a buying family on the school clock:** You already feel the pressure, so let me be honest rather than alarming: the homes that fit your criteria are the same ones every other relocating family wants, and selection tightens with every week of summer. The advantage goes to families who get representation before they get on the plane — with financing arranged, school applications running in parallel, and access to coming-soon and off-market opportunities. The families who treat the home search and the school search as one coordinated project land softly. The ones who sequence them run out of runway.

**If your timeline is flexible:** Then the deadline isn't yours, and you shouldn't compete as if it is. Buyers without school pressure may find better leverage in the early-fall lull, after the deadline-driven wave closes. Urgency is real for a specific buyer — it doesn't have to be manufactured for everyone.

## The Bottom Line

Thirteen years ago, I made the move from New York myself, and I remember exactly what these families are managing — the career, the kids, the boxes, the countdown. The firms came first. The families followed. And now the school calendar has compressed their decisions into a summer window that is keeping every serious realtor in Boca Raton fully occupied.

This isn't a moment that rewards waiting on the sidelines — for sellers with the right product or for families staring down an enrollment deadline. The market is moving now because families have to move now.

For buyers and real estate professionals alike, the takeaway is simple: in Boca Raton's club communities, the school calendar has become the market calendar.
