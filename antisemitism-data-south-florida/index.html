Antisemitism, Migration, and South Florida: What the Data Shows — and What It Doesn't

By Edmund Bogen · Douglas Elliman Real Estate · July 22, 2026

Most weeks, this space is about inventory, absorption rates, and pricing. This week I want to take on a harder subject — one that comes up in national headlines, in community conversations, and increasingly in questions about why people move. I'm going to treat it the way I treat everything else: with data, with sources, and with honesty about what the numbers can and cannot tell us.

The National Picture

The Anti-Defamation League has tracked antisemitic incidents in the United States for decades. Its annual Audit counts harassment, vandalism, and assault — including conduct that is offensive but not criminal. The trend since 2019 is stark: 2,107 incidents in 2019; 3,697 in 2022; 8,873 in 2023; 9,354 in 2024 — the highest total ever recorded by the ADL; and 6,274 in 2025 — a decline, but still roughly three times the 2019 level and the third-highest total on record.

Two important caveats. First, the ADL changed its methodology after October 7, 2023, to include certain anti-Zionist and pro-"resistance" expressions in specific contexts — ADL itself reports that 1,350 of the 8,873 incidents in 2023 were included because of that change (excluding them, the total would be 7,523). General criticism of Israeli policy is not automatically counted. Second, these are incidents recorded by the ADL through victim reports, media, and partner organizations — not criminal statistics.

The criminal data come from the FBI and Department of Justice, which count only offenses motivated by bias, as reported by participating law-enforcement agencies. That narrower series shows the same direction: anti-Jewish hate-crime incidents rose from 953 in 2019 to 1,122 in 2022 to 1,832 in 2023. The FBI's 2024 dataset, released in August 2025, recorded 11,679 hate-crime incidents across all bias categories; 2025 figures had not yet been released as of this writing.

Different datasets, different definitions — but the same conclusion. The rise in antisemitism in America since 2019 is real, documented, and significant.

The Florida Picture

Florida follows the national pattern. ADL-recorded incidents in the state rose from 91 in 2019 to a peak of 463 in 2023, then eased to 353 in 2024 and 319 in 2025 — still more than triple the 2019 baseline. Palm Beach County recorded 51 incidents in 2024, the highest county total in the state, and rose to 71 in 2025, when Palm Beach and Miami-Dade together accounted for 43% of Florida's incidents. In 2025, Florida also ranked second nationally in incidents targeting private residences. One note of discipline: these are raw counts, not population-adjusted risk rates, and they say nothing about the relative safety of any city or neighborhood.

Anyone who tells you Florida is immune to this problem isn't reading the data. It is a serious issue here, as it is nationally.

How People Are Responding

The American Jewish Committee's State of Antisemitism surveys measure something the incident counts can't: how people feel and how they've changed their behavior. In 2024, 56% of American Jews reported avoiding at least one behavior — wearing identifying items, attending events, posting online — out of concern about antisemitism, and that figure held at 55% in 2025. Also in 2025, 17% said they had considered leaving the United States altogether because of antisemitism within the prior five years. Considered — an attitude, not a completed move — but a striking number all the same.

Those are sobering numbers. They describe a community that feels less safe than it did a few years ago, and that is factoring safety into real decisions.

Now the Part Most Commentary Gets Wrong

Here is where I have to be more careful than the average headline writer — because the honest answer is more nuanced than the popular narrative.

Is there evidence that antisemitism is measurably driving migration to South Florida, or demand in our housing market? In every public dataset I reviewed as of July 21, 2026, the answer is no — no methodologically documented source establishes that link.

The best evidence we have is the 2024 Jewish Miami community study conducted by Brandeis University and NORC. It found that a quarter of Jewish adults in Miami arrived within the past decade — real, substantial in-migration. But when newcomers were asked why they moved, the leading answers were family and friends (39%), weather (37%), career (29%), the character of the local community (26%), and finances (21%). Antisemitism was selected by 3% — a published answer, but far from a leading one. And the study measured stated reasons for moving to Miami-Dade; it did not measure home purchases, and its findings can't simply be extended to Boca Raton or Palm Beach County.

South Florida's Jewish community was also large and growing long before 2023. A 2018 Brandeis study estimated roughly 134,200 Jewish residents in the South Palm Beach County federation area alone. And the broadest migration datasets — IRS state-to-state flows, national mover studies — continue to show the same drivers they've shown for years: family, jobs, retirement, lifestyle, taxes, and weather.

Could concerns about safety be one factor among many for some households? That's plausible, and surveys suggest safety weighs on people's minds. But "plausible" is not the same as "proven," and I'm not going to present an inference as a fact.

Why I'm Being This Careful

There's also a professional principle at stake. Fair-housing law — federal and Florida — prohibits characterizing homes or neighborhoods by religion, and the REALTOR® Code of Ethics prohibits steering buyers toward or away from communities based on protected characteristics. Those rules exist for good reasons, and they're why you will never hear me market a property or a neighborhood based on who I think should live there. Every buyer defines their own criteria; my job is data, access, and execution.

The Bottom Line

The rise in antisemitism since 2019 is one of the most thoroughly documented social trends in America, and it deserves to be taken seriously — in Florida as much as anywhere. What the data does not yet support is the popular claim that it is remaking South Florida's housing market. Migration here remains driven by the fundamentals it has always been driven by. When the evidence changes, I'll report it. Until then, you'll get the numbers straight — that's the deal.
