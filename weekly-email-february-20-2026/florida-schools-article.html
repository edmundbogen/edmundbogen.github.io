New York City and California are among the nation's biggest spenders on public education. New York State spends roughly $29,900 per student in current expenditures (NCES FY2022), with 2024–25 budgets now exceeding $36,000. New York City's education department budgets more than $38,000 per student, with some estimates exceeding $42,000 when all funding sources are included. California spends approximately $23,800 per student (NCES FY2022), rising to an estimated $25,900 in FY2023. Florida, by contrast, spends around $11,800 per student in current expenditures—less than half of New York's outlay and roughly half of California's.

Yet in the most recent Nation's Report Card (NAEP) assessments, Florida's fourth-grade students ranked among the top states in reading and math, while New York and California posted mid-pack results. This paradox invites a question: if it isn't just about money, what is Florida doing differently?

The purpose of this analysis is to unpack that paradox for parents, voters and business owners. We will compare per-pupil spending in Florida, California and New York City/New York State, evaluate their academic outcomes, explore the policies that set Florida apart, diagnose why high-spending states are not getting better results, and draw practical lessons that leaders elsewhere can adopt without mimicking Florida's politics. All statistics are approximate and directional; where precise numbers are not available, the emphasis is on relative differences.

Florida's per-pupil spending is so low that states like New York and California could effectively fund two or even three students for every Florida pupil with the same dollars. Despite this, Florida often outperforms the high-spending states on key elementary academic measures—though the picture is more complicated at the secondary level.

The Results: How Kids Are Actually Doing

The National Assessment of Educational Progress (NAEP) is the only nationwide exam that allows apples-to-apples comparisons across states and large districts.

In 2024, Florida's fourth-grade students scored 218 in reading and 243 in math. Both figures were higher than the national averages of 214 and 237, respectively. About 33% of Florida's fourth-graders reached reading proficiency, and 45% reached math proficiency—placing Florida among the top five states at the elementary level.

However, a critical caveat: Florida's 8th grade picture is far less rosy. On the 2024 NAEP, Florida's eighth-graders ranked 41st to 43rd nationally, with reading scores dropping 7 points and math scores hitting a 20-year low. Researchers at Stanford's Center for Education Policy Analysis (CEPA) have found that Florida students learn approximately 12% less per year between 3rd and 8th grade compared to the national average. This suggests that while Florida's early-grade interventions produce strong 4th grade results, sustaining those gains through middle school remains a significant challenge.

Methodological note: Florida's mandatory 3rd grade retention policy, which holds back students who do not meet reading proficiency standards, means that the state's 4th grade NAEP cohort excludes some of the lowest-performing students who are repeating 3rd grade. While the policy itself has merit as an intervention, researchers note that it can mechanically inflate 4th grade scores relative to states without such policies.

For families relocating to Florida, this underscores the importance of evaluating individual school performance at all grade levels rather than relying solely on statewide elementary metrics.

California's NAEP results were middling. Fourth-grade reading averaged 212—about the national average—and 56% of students were at or above the basic level. Fourth-grade math averaged 233 and 35% were proficient. Eighth-grade reading scored 254, roughly matching national performance, and about 28% were proficient. Eighth-grade math scored 269 (slightly below the national average) with 25% proficient.

"In a demographically-adjusted ranking using NAEP data, the Urban Institute found that Florida ranked 3rd overall while New York ranked 18th and California 19th."

New York's statewide data are not as easily accessible for 2024, but local and national analyses put the state in the middle of the pack. A Citizens Budget Commission summary noted that New York's NAEP performance in 2024 ranked 32nd in fourth-grade reading and 46th in fourth-grade math and 9th and 22nd in eighth-grade reading and math, respectively. Florida's strong elementary results contrast sharply with New York's middling outcomes despite spending twice as much per student.

Within New York City, the 2024 snapshot tells a similar story. Fourth-grade students in New York City scored 209 in reading—on par with large urban districts—and just 28% were proficient. In math, the city's fourth-grade score of 230 still lagged the national average, with 33% meeting the proficient benchmark. Eighth-grade proficiency rates were roughly 23% in math and 29% in reading.

Performance of Disadvantaged Students

Florida's policies appear especially beneficial for low-income and minority students. A 2024 American Enterprise Institute (AEI) report on “Grading Florida’s Schools” concluded that Florida delivers the best educational return on investment in the nation and is “the single best state in which to be a low-income public school student.” (Note: AEI is a center-right policy institute. The Urban Institute, a center-left research organization, has reached similar conclusions about Florida’s demographically adjusted elementary performance, lending cross-ideological support to the finding.)

"Florida delivers the best educational return on investment in the nation and is the single best state in which to be a low-income public school student."

The report emphasised that Florida's fourth-grade students ranked third nationally in reading and fourth in math and that at-risk subgroups maintained high performance during the pandemic.

Florida's success also shows up in post-secondary outcomes. An EdChoice research review noted that participation in Florida's low-income tax-credit scholarship program increased college enrollment by 12% for elementary and middle school scholarship recipients and by 19% for high-school recipients. A separate study found that the program improved math and reading scores and reduced absenteeism and suspensions among students who remained in public schools.

What Florida Is Doing Differently

Florida's relatively strong outcomes come from deliberate policy choices rather than luck. Four themes—accountability and transparency, early literacy, school choice and funding equity—distinguish the Sunshine State.

Accountability Through A–F School Grades

Florida pioneered an A–F school grading system in the late 1990s. Schools receive a letter grade based on student achievement and growth, with extra weight for the lowest-performing students. ExcelinEd's policy playbook explains that such grading systems provide parents with simple, transparent information and create powerful incentives for improvement. Evidence from Florida shows that low-performing schools responded to the threat of a failing grade by making real gains—not just "teaching to the test"—and that accountability systems correlate with improved test scores. Unlike vague labels or descriptors used elsewhere, an A-to-F scale forces administrators and communities to confront underperformance and rewards progress.

Early Literacy: Reading by Third Grade

Florida invests heavily in early reading. The state's "Read by Grade 3" policy requires schools to screen K–3 pupils, provide intensive interventions for struggling readers and retain students who cannot demonstrate adequate reading skills by the end of third grade. Florida law states that if a third-grade student's reading deficiency is not remediated, the child must be retained unless a "good cause" exemption applies. The same statute eliminates social promotion: students may not be promoted beyond third grade without demonstrating reading proficiency.

This strong stance aims to ensure that virtually all students are reading proficiently before moving into upper elementary grades. An implementation review notes that Florida's comprehensive early literacy strategy—supporting K–3 interventions and retention—has been key to its gains in reading.

A Robust School Choice Ecosystem

Florida has one of the most extensive school-choice landscapes in the country. Nearly 500,000 students attend private schools on funded scholarships and another 155,000 are homeschooled—numbers that dwarf many states' entire K–12 enrollment. Florida offers tax-credit scholarships, education savings accounts, charter schools and open enrollment options. Recent legislation made scholarships universal, removing most income restrictions while prioritising lower-income families.

Parents enthusiastically support these programs; an EdChoice survey reported that over 90% of Florida parents using the scholarship program said their children now try their best, stay out of trouble, pay attention and feel safe at their chosen schools. Research also suggests that the competitive pressure from vouchers and charters improves public-school outcomes: the Florida Tax Credit Scholarship program led to better math and reading scores and reduced absenteeism among students who remained in public schools.

Accountability remains a work in progress. A 2024 state audit identified a $270 million gap in scholarship program oversight, including $47 million paid for students enrolled in the wrong schools. Additionally, approximately 69% of new voucher recipients in recent years were students who had never attended a public school, raising questions about whether the program is primarily expanding access or subsidizing families already choosing private education. These findings highlight the need for continued program refinement even as the model expands.

Funding Equity: Targeting Dollars to Students

Unlike many states, Florida's main funding formula—the Florida Education Finance Program (FEFP)—aims to equalise opportunities across districts. Enacted in 1973, the FEFP recognizes differences in local property wealth, program costs and sparsity. It funds students based on a weighted full-time equivalent (FTE) calculation rather than on teacher headcount or district budgets. Weighted FTE are multiplied by a base student allocation and adjusted for local cost-of-living to determine state and local funding.

This design ensures that districts with weak tax bases or high concentrations of special-needs students receive additional aid while preventing wealthy districts from hoarding resources. In practice, Florida's formula channels dollars to interventions such as reading coaches, intensive tutoring and extended learning time rather than to administrative overhead.

Culture and Consistency

Florida's reforms have persisted across administrations. Since Jeb Bush's governorship in the late 1990s, successive governors and legislatures have maintained an outcomes-driven agenda focusing on early literacy, progress monitoring and school choice. The AEI report credits this continuity for Florida's sustained improvement, noting that at-risk subgroups weathered the pandemic better than those in most other states. A culture of measuring results and acting on them—rather than simply increasing budgets—has taken root.

However, Florida’s position as the lowest-paying state for teachers (50th nationally per the NEA) creates real recruitment and retention challenges, with teacher real wages declining 12.9% over the past decade. For families evaluating school quality, the ability of individual schools to attract and retain talented educators may vary significantly.

Because many of these benefit costs finance legacy pension debt, they do little to improve current students’ education.

High-spending states have not just higher pay; they also employ more adults. The Reason Foundation notes that many states, including New York, added non-teaching staff even as enrollment declined. Large central offices and complex collective-bargaining agreements limit flexibility. A Cato Institute analysis observed that New York spent $29,284 per pupil yet achieved no better results than lower-spending states like Utah or Idaho, blaming the disparity on bloated bureaucracies and rigid union rules. The Citizens Budget Commission found that despite spending 91% above the national average, New York's NAEP performance remained mediocre.

California faces similar cost pressures. The Public Policy Institute of California reports that per-pupil current spending was roughly $23,800–$25,900 in recent years, and 80% of that went to staffing. Rising pension contributions consumed about 25% of the pre-pandemic spending increase between 2013–14 and 2019–20. Once labor costs are adjusted for the state's high cost of living, California drops from 16th to 31st in per-pupil spending ranking—meaning the extra dollars mainly cover higher prices rather than more services.

The physical environments in New York City and California also drive costs higher. The Citizens Budget Commission found that building new schools in New York City cost about $100,000 per seat and that the city spent $9.1 billion constructing new schools between 2005 and 2018. California faces high construction costs driven by seismic standards, labor rules and a housing market that inflates land prices. Florida, by contrast, relies on horizontal, campus-style buildings that are cheaper to construct and maintain.

Both New York and California have layered governance structures—state departments, city districts, county offices—and frequent political swings. Negotiating reforms requires coordination across school boards, teachers' unions and multiple levels of government. This complexity can water down accountability initiatives. In contrast, Florida's state-centric model allows for more direct policy implementation and consistency over time.

What New York and California Could Learn

No two states are alike—Florida's demographics, politics and labor markets differ from those of California and New York. Still, several principles emerge from Florida's experience.

  • Focus on measurable outcomes, not just inputs. Replace opaque accountability systems with clear, public ratings. Florida's A–F grades show that transparency can spur improvement.
  • Strengthen early literacy policies. Adopt "reading by third grade" statutes that require screening, evidence-based interventions and retention only when necessary.
  • Expand meaningful school choice. Allow charter schools, tax-credit scholarships and education savings accounts to create competition and give families options.
  • Target dollars to students rather than systems. Move toward student-weighted funding formulas that account for poverty, disability and English-learner status.
  • Provide consistent leadership. Develop bipartisan consensus around goals to avoid seesaw reforms.
  • Reform pensions and benefits. Address unsustainable pension liabilities so more dollars reach classrooms.
  • Increase transparency for parents and taxpayers. Publish easy-to-understand report cards and dashboards showing school performance, spending and outcomes.

Adopting these principles does not mean copying Florida wholesale. Each state must adapt reforms to local conditions and legal frameworks. But the evidence suggests that focusing on policy design, incentives and execution delivers greater returns than simply spending more.

It's Not Just About Money

Florida’s public schools illustrate that how money is spent matters more than how much. The state invests less per pupil than high-spending jurisdictions yet outperforms them in elementary grades and provides strong returns for low-income students. Its success is rooted in policies that emphasise accountability, early literacy, choice, equitable funding and consistent leadership. At the same time, Florida’s 8th grade results reveal that sustaining early gains through middle school remains an unsolved challenge—one the state must address to fully validate its model.

In contrast, New York City and California spend enormous sums but face high labor costs, pension burdens, costly facilities and fragmented governance, leaving them with middling outcomes. For policymakers and taxpayers, the lesson is clear: more dollars alone will not fix schools. The challenge is to design systems that align incentives with student learning and invest in what works—while ensuring those gains endure beyond elementary school.
