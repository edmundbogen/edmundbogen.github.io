St. Andrews at the Crossroads

A Data Brief on the Proposed Clubhouse Rebuild and Home Values Inside the Gates  ·  May 5, 2026  ·  By Edmund Bogen

The lede I keep coming back to

In October 2023, 7234 Ayrshire Lane — a 1984-built home inside St. Andrews — sold for $2,020,000 at $471 per square foot. Eighteen months later, in March 2025, the same address closed again at $10,500,000 at $1,292 per square foot — this time as a 2025 ground-up new construction.

Same dirt. Same gate. 5.2x the price.

That single comp tells you most of what you need to know about where the St. Andrews market actually sits today. The structure on the lot is depreciating. The land — and what you're allowed to build on it — is the asset. And buyers are paying a premium that, on a per-square-foot basis, is now roughly 50% above the rest of the original-condition stock inside the gates.

This piece is my best attempt at an honest, balanced look at what the numbers say about the proposed major capital program at St. Andrews — including a full demolition and ground-up rebuild of the clubhouse — and what comparable clubs have actually experienced when they've taken on programs of this scale. I'm a member here. I also list and sell here. Both perspectives shape what I think matters in this conversation, and I've tried to lay the data out so members can draw their own conclusions.

I am deliberately not publishing a project cost figure. Final scope, contingency, and assessment structure are still being refined by the club's leadership. What I can do is show you what peer clubs have spent, what their per-square-foot economics looked like, and what happened to home values during and after their programs.

What's actually being proposed

The headline change is straightforward to describe: a complete demolition of the existing clubhouse and a ground-up rebuild — not a renovation, not a refresh — alongside a golf course program. The exact square footage, amenity mix, and final budget are still being shaped, and the figures circulating among members vary widely. I'm not going to anchor on any single number until the membership package is final.

What I will say is that the question facing St. Andrews is not unusual. Across South Florida and the country, peer private clubs have run major capital programs in the last five years — some larger, some smaller — and the outcomes are well-documented enough that members here can reason from comparable evidence rather than from speculation.

A few facts that frame the conversation:

  • St. Andrews has 728 lots inside the gates.
  • Current initiation cost is $500,000 total — $450,000 to the club plus $50,000 to the POA.
  • The club has been operating in a 1980s-vintage clubhouse footprint and infrastructure that has been refreshed but not rebuilt.

Whatever the final number, the program will be funded through some combination of assessment, financing, and member resources. The questions members are asking — Will this affect my home value? Will it affect carrying cost? Is the timing right? — are exactly the right questions. The numbers below are the ones I'd want to look at if I were on either side of the decision.

The headline takeaway: a roughly 50% per-square-foot premium for new or fully-renovated homes versus dated stock, with a 3.4x spread between the lowest and highest $/sqft closings inside the gates.

Read another way: 28 of the last 97 closings — about 29% of recent volume — happened in the new-or-renovated cohort. The remaining 71% closed at dated-stock $/sqft levels. The market is not pricing the gate alone; it is pricing the gate plus the condition of the structure on the lot.

This is the bifurcation that any major capital program needs to be evaluated against. A new clubhouse does not change the year built on a 1985 home. What it can do — based on peer-club experience — is move the floor of the gate's value proposition, and over time, narrow or widen the cohort spread depending on how the program is executed.

Days-on-market is the other number worth watching. The 2023 frenzy compressed DOM to a 20-day median; 2024 normalized to 66 days; 2025 settled at 49 days. That's a market that is liquid, not euphoric — buyers are taking time, but the right product is moving.

A few honest observations from the peer set:

  1. $/sqft for full ground-up clubhouse rebuilds in this asset class is currently running roughly $600 – $950 per square foot, with the higher end reflecting more elaborate amenity programs, post-pandemic construction inflation, and complex sites.
  2. Contingency allowances of 15–20% are now standard in private-club capital programs of this size, given materials volatility and labor pricing in 2024–2026.
  3. Cost overruns happen, and they happen often enough that any membership conversation about a rebuild should include a clear discussion of who absorbs them. Oakland Hills, for example, ran materially above its initial budget; so have several other clubs quietly.

I include those facts not to scare anyone — peer clubs have generally come through these programs with stronger amenity offerings and stronger membership demand on the other side — but because the membership conversation is a healthier one when those numbers are visible up front rather than discovered late.

How clubhouse rebuilds typically affect home values

Members reasonably want to know: what happens to home values during and after a major capital program?

The pattern across peer clubs that have run programs of this scale tends to look something like this:

During construction (Year 0 – Year 2 typically)

  • Days-on-market often expands. Buyers ask harder questions about ongoing assessments, dust, noise, and amenity disruption.
  • Sale prices on dated-stock homes can soften modestly, particularly for sellers who need to move during peak construction phases.
  • The new-and-renovated cohort tends to be more resilient because those buyers are less price-sensitive and more amenity-focused.
  • Members who can defer a sale until the program completes generally do.

Post-completion (Year 2 onward)

  • Documented lift in the new-and-renovated cohort, where the new amenity package supports the buyer narrative.
  • Compression in the spread between dated and renovated stock — but only if the dated cohort responds with renovation activity. If owners hold without modernizing, the spread can actually widen.
  • Increased buyer inquiry from out-of-market luxury buyers, who use peer-club rebuilds as a signal that the community is investing in itself.

I want to be careful here: none of this is guaranteed. The peer-club outcomes I've reviewed are documented but not deterministic. Execution matters. Governance matters. Whether the program comes in close to budget matters. And the broader luxury market matters. A clubhouse rebuild does not insulate a community from a national luxury correction.

What I will say is that, across the peer set, the clubs that ran these programs with strong member communication, transparent assessments, and disciplined contingency generally came out the other side with stronger pricing and tighter inventory. The clubs that didn't are still working through it.

Frictions and risks worth naming

A balanced piece names the frictions, not just the upside.

1. Initiation and total cost of entry. $500,000 in initiation ($450K club + $50K POA) is a real number. For buyers entering at the dated-stock price tier ($1.5M – $2.5M), the initiation is 20–30% of acquisition price. For buyers in the new-construction tier ($7M – $10M), it's roughly 5–7% of acquisition. The program's economics will land differently across those cohorts.

2. Assessment uncertainty. Until the membership package is final, individual member exposure isn't fully knowable. Members making short-term decisions on whether to renovate, sell, or hold should ask the club directly for the most current scope and assessment guidance.

3. Contingency and overruns. As noted above, peer-club programs of this size have run 15–20% contingency. Whether that proves sufficient depends on materials pricing, labor availability, and scope discipline through the build.

4. Governance precedent. It's worth knowing that the Carolina Golf Club (Charlotte, NC) became a public case study after a major capital program ran ~33% over budget. The club's membership voted 229-166 to proceed with revised assessments, and a member lawsuit challenging the process was dismissed in January 2026. Most members report being satisfied with the outcome today. The takeaway isn't "don't do it" — it's "communicate early and often, and document the assumptions."

5. The dated-stock owner's question. If you own an original-condition 1980s home inside the gates, the most consequential decision in front of you may not be how to vote on the clubhouse program — it may be whether to renovate, sell as a renovation candidate, or hold and accept the spread. The MLS data above shows that this is already the largest delta inside the gates today.

What I'd tell a member, what I'd tell a buyer

As a member: read the package carefully when it arrives, ask for the contingency assumptions, and weigh the program against your personal timeline. If you're planning to be at the club for 10+ years, the calculus is different than if you're three years from a move. There is no single right answer.

The bifurcation is the story. Renovated and new-construction homes are commanding $982 – $1,031 per square foot at the median. Dated, original-condition homes are clearing $661. That spread exists today, before the rebuild conversation reaches its conclusion. Whatever happens with the capital program, that spread will keep mattering.

As a buyer: the same bifurcation is your opportunity. You can buy into the gate at $1.5M – $2.5M with a renovation budget, at $5M – $7M for fully-renovated existing stock, or at $7M – $10.5M for new construction. Three different cost bases. Three different return profiles. All inside the same 728-lot community.
