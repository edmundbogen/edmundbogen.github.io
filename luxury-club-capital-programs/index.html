When a Country Club Rebuilds: What the Data Says About Home Values

An analytical framework for members and buyers facing major capital programs in South Florida's luxury private-club communities — drawn from the closed-sales record, peer-club benchmarks, and the patterns that separate the rebuilds that work from the ones that don't.
May 5, 2026  ·  By Edmund Bogen

A tale of two country clubs

Across South Florida and the country, some private clubs run major capital programs and emerge on the other side with stronger amenities, stronger demand, and stronger home values. Other clubs spend comparable money — and the lift never arrives. Beautiful facilities, depleted reserves, declining membership, soft real estate. In a handful of well-known cases over the last fifteen years, capital programs have been followed by sales of land for redevelopment, mergers, or quiet financial restructurings.

The honest reading of the peer-club record is not "rebuilds always work." It is: execution, governance, and macro timing decide which version of the story a club ends up writing. That is the question on the table at any club currently weighing a major capital program — not whether a new clubhouse could add value, but whether the program, as it gets finalized, lands on the side of the ledger that produces lift.

This piece is a framework — built from the closed-sales record, peer-club benchmarks, and the patterns I have watched across the luxury private-club market in South Florida over the last decade. It is meant for members weighing assessment decisions, buyers weighing entry, and anyone trying to think clearly about the economics of the country-club rebuild question.

Disclosure before the data

I owe you the disclosure before the analysis.

I am a member of a South Florida private equity club, and I am a listing agent who works across twenty-one luxury communities in Palm Beach and Broward counties. The teardown signal: one comp, not a market

Eighteen months later, the same lot closed again at roughly $1,290 per square foot — this time as a 2025 ground-up new construction. Same dirt. Same gate. Roughly 5x the price per foot.

A few honest observations from the peer set:

  1. $/sqft for full ground-up clubhouse rebuilds in this asset class is currently running roughly $600 – $950 per square foot, with the higher end reflecting more elaborate amenity programs, post-pandemic construction inflation, and complex sites.
  2. Contingency allowances of 15–20% are now standard in private-club capital programs of this size, given materials volatility and labor pricing in 2024–2026.
  3. Cost overruns happen. One widely-reported public case: the Carolina Golf Club in Charlotte, NC, where a major capital program ran approximately 33% over budget. Membership voted 229-166 to proceed with revised assessments, and a member lawsuit challenging the process was dismissed in January 2026. The club is operating today and most members report being satisfied with the outcome — but the path was contentious.

I include those facts not to scare anyone — peer clubs that execute well have generally come through these programs with stronger amenity offerings and stronger membership demand on the other side — but because the membership conversation is a healthier one when those numbers are visible up front rather than discovered late.

How clubhouse rebuilds typically affect home values

Members and buyers reasonably want to know: what happens to home values during and after a major capital program?

The pattern across peer clubs that have run programs of this scale tends to look something like this:

During construction (Year 0 – Year 2 typically)

  • Days-on-market often expands. Buyers ask harder questions about ongoing assessments, dust, noise, and amenity disruption.
  • Sale prices on dated-stock homes can soften modestly, particularly for sellers who need to move during peak construction phases.
  • The new-and-renovated cohort tends to be more resilient because those buyers are less price-sensitive and more amenity-focused.
  • Members who can defer a sale until the program completes generally do.

Post-completion (Year 2 onward)

  • Documented lift in the new-and-renovated cohort, where the new amenity package supports the buyer narrative.
  • Compression in the spread between dated and renovated stock — but only if the dated cohort responds with renovation activity. If owners hold without modernizing, the spread can actually widen.
  • Increased buyer inquiry from out-of-market luxury buyers, who use peer-club rebuilds as a signal that the community is investing in itself.

I want to be careful here: none of this is guaranteed. The peer-club outcomes I have reviewed are documented but not deterministic. Execution matters. Governance matters. Whether the program comes in close to budget matters. And the broader luxury market matters. A clubhouse rebuild does not insulate a community from a national luxury correction.

What I will say is that, across the peer set, the clubs that ran these programs with strong member communication, transparent assessments, and disciplined contingency generally came out the other side with stronger pricing and tighter inventory. The clubs that didn't are still working through it.

Frictions and risks worth naming

A balanced framework names the frictions, not just the upside.

1. Initiation and total cost of entry. In the major Boca Raton private clubs, member initiation costs typically range from $200,000 to over $500,000, depending on the club. For buyers entering at the dated-stock price tier, initiation can represent 15–30% of acquisition cost. For buyers in the new-construction tier, it can drop to 5–7%. The economics of any capital program land differently across those cohorts.

2. Assessment uncertainty. Until a club's membership package is final, individual member exposure is not fully knowable. Members making short-term decisions on whether to renovate, sell, or hold should ask the club directly for the most current scope and assessment guidance.

3. Contingency and overruns. As noted above, peer-club programs of this size have run 15–20% contingency. Whether that proves sufficient depends on materials pricing, labor availability, and scope discipline through the build.

4. Governance precedent. The Carolina Golf Club case noted above is the most-cited public example of how a contested capital program can play out — overrun, vote, lawsuit, dismissal, eventual completion. The takeaway isn't "don't do it." It's "communicate early and often, and document the assumptions."

5. The dated-stock owner's question. If you own an original-condition 1980s home inside one of these gates, the most consequential decision in front of you may not be how to vote on a capital program — it may be whether to renovate, sell as a renovation candidate, or hold and accept the cohort spread. The closed-sales record shows that this is already the largest delta inside these communities today.

What if the membership says no, or scales it back

The framework would not be honest if it only modeled the build scenario. There are three plausible outcomes any membership can land on:

  1. Full ground-up rebuild as proposed. Highest peer-club benchmark cost. Highest disruption window. Highest theoretical lift if execution is clean and the macro luxury market cooperates.
  2. Phased or scaled-back program. Lower assessment exposure, lower disruption, more modest amenity lift. Several peer clubs have run multi-phase programs rather than a single ground-up rebuild.
  3. Status quo / renovation-only. The clubhouse continues to be refreshed within its existing footprint. No major assessment, no major disruption. Also: no amenity reset, and continued aging of older infrastructure.

Each path carries a different cost basis and a different risk profile. None of the three options is risk-free. Status quo is not "no decision" — it is a decision to let existing infrastructure continue to age, with the inventory and amenity consequences that follow. Full rebuild is not a guaranteed lift — it is a bet on execution, contingency discipline, and the macro luxury market through the build window. Phased programs sit in between on both axes.

The question for any membership is rarely "rebuild yes or no." It is which version of which path, on what timeline, with what assessment structure, and with what governance discipline through the build.

The four objections most worth taking seriously

Across the conversations I have had with members weighing capital programs, the most thoughtful skeptical questions tend to fall into four categories. They are worth naming because a balanced framework does not just list risks in the abstract — it acknowledges what real members are actually saying.

  • "The existing footprint has been refreshed, not rebuilt — and the refreshes have generally worked." Members at long-standing clubs have often watched the clubhouse improved multiple times over decades. The argument is that a more incremental approach has a track record, and that a ground-up rebuild is a step change in cost and disruption that may not be necessary.
  • "The dated-cohort owners are being asked to underwrite the new-construction cohort's amenity package." If a program lifts $/sqft most strongly in the new-and-renovated cohort, while the assessment lands as a much higher percentage of the dated cohort's home value, that is a real cross-subsidy. It does not make a program wrong, but it deserves to be named.
  • "Macro timing is uncertain." Construction inflation has cooled but not reversed. The luxury market has held but is not euphoric. A rebuild that runs into a softer macro environment can produce the disruption without the lift, particularly if it overlaps with a national luxury correction.
  • "Governance is the variable." Members who have watched peer-club programs note that the difference between the clubs that thrived and the clubs that didn't was rarely the architecture. It was the governance — the contingency discipline, the communication cadence, the willingness to slow down or restage when the numbers shifted. Many members want to see that governance posture demonstrated before, not after, the vote.

I include these because they are the questions any well-designed membership package should answer directly. None of them is a reason not to do a program. All of them are reasons to do one well.

What I'd tell a member, what I'd tell a buyer

As a member of any club facing this decision: read the package carefully when it arrives. Ask for the contingency assumptions. Ask how cohort asymmetry is being handled. Ask what the governance posture is on overruns. Weigh the program against your personal timeline. If you are planning to be at the club for ten or more years, the calculus is different than if you are three years from a move. There is no single right answer for every member.

The bifurcation is the story. Renovated and new-construction homes across these communities are commanding $900–$1,300 per square foot at the upper end. Dated, original-condition homes are clearing in the $500s and $600s. That spread exists today, before any rebuild conversation reaches its conclusion. Whatever any membership decides, that spread will keep mattering — and how a program is structured determines whether it narrows, holds, or widens.

As a buyer evaluating one of these communities: ask the same questions a member would ask. The right entry point depends on your appetite for the assessment exposure, the disruption window, and the cohort you are buying into. I would not tell you that any one cost basis is the right one without knowing your timeline.
